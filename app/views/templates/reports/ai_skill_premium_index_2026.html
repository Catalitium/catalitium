Executive Summary

  • ›Postings mentioning at least one AI skill show about a 28% salary premium (~$18,000/year); two or more AI skills ~43%.
  • ›In Big Tech and well-funded startups, AI/ML engineers earn about 20–22% more total comp than comparable SWEs (e.g. $262K vs $215K entry; $445K vs $370K staff).
  • ›Public companies: ML engineers ~20% base premium over SWEs, plus 10–20% equity uplift for AI roles.
  • ›LLM engineers ~25–40% above general ML; MLOps ~20–35% above general ML.
  • ›AI safety / alignment ~45% compensation growth since 2023; AI professionals median ~$160K; senior ML engineers ~$212,928.

Section 8: Myths vs Reality

Myth 1: Any engineer who adds “AI” earns ~30% more

Reality: Postings data supports ~28–43% for matching skills; keywords alone do not guarantee a jump. Confidence: High.

Myth 2: Classical ML is worthless in the LLM era

Reality: Sr ML engineers (~$185.9K) vs sr data scientists (~$152.7K) ~22% premium; classical ML remains foundational. Confidence: Medium.

Myth 3: India vs US gap after PPP is settled

Reality: Not answerable with robust public PPP-adjusted AI-specific series. Confidence: Low.

Myth 4: Premiums will compress quickly as supply grows

Reality: Partly true for generic skills; deep specializations stay materially above SWE baselines. Confidence: Medium–high.

Section 9: Recommendations

Job seekers

  • Invest in depth (LLM fine-tuning, MLOps, infra) vs surface literacy.
  • Track safety/alignment trajectory and pool size.
  • Weigh geographic arbitrage vs PPP and retention.

Recruiters

  • Separate keyword density from verified depth ($18K–$50K+ spread).
  • Expect longer fills for LLM, safety, MLOps outside major hubs.
  • Benchmark vs Big Tech and late-stage startup ceilings.

Companies

  • Structure bands for 20–40% AI premium without distorting core engineering.
  • Compete on mission and velocity if not on $2M+ equity.
  • Commission local surveys for EU/India where data is thin.

Closing Statement

AI skill premiums are real and measurable, but not monolithic: from ~5% for generic familiarity to 40%+ for deep LLM and safety expertise. Treating AI as one undifferentiated skill category leads to bad hiring, bad careers, and bad investments.
