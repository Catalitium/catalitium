Executive Summary

This report examines whether autonomous AI Agents as a Service (AaaS) are on track to capture at least 30 percent of enterprise software spend by 2028 by displacing traditional SaaS through lower total cost of ownership (TCO) and higher automation. Evidence from Gartner, IDC, McKinsey, Automation Anywhere, and emerging agent platforms shows rapid enterprise experimentation with agentic AI, material cost reductions of 30–50 percent in targeted workflows, and early shifts in budget allocation from licenses to automation outcomes. If these trends continue, AaaS is likely to become the default interface for many operational workflows by the late 2020s, forcing SaaS vendors to pivot from selling seats to orchestrating and operating agents on behalf of customers.

Key Findings

  1. Agentic AI is moving into enterprise core spend, but major analysts do not yet break out “AaaS” as its own line item. IDC forecasts worldwide enterprise application revenue reaching 483.1 billion USD in 2027, with more than two-thirds from public cloud, largely SaaS. Gartner reports SaaS as the largest cloud segment (247.2 billion USD in 2024, ~20% YoY). AI-centric systems spending grows much faster (~27% annually 2022–2026 in cited guides), with agentic AI expected to drive a growing share of enterprise application revenue over time.
  2. GenAI and agentic systems are already pulling single- to low-double-digit shares of SaaS-adjacent budgets. Commentary and surveys cite enterprise generative AI spend rising from 1.7 billion USD in 2023 to 37 billion USD in 2025, on the order of ~6% of the global SaaS market over that window, with most use cases bought rather than built in-house. IDC estimated enterprise investment in generative AI solutions above 19.5 billion USD in 2023.
  3. Gartner expects deep penetration of agentic AI in applications: ~40% of enterprise applications integrated with task-specific AI agents by 2026 (up from a low single-digit base), and agentic AI accounting for roughly 30% of enterprise application software revenue by 2035. By 2028, about a third of user experiences may shift from native apps to agentic front ends.
  4. Workflow-level TCO evidence supports large savings in narrow domains. Automation Anywhere reports AI service agents resolving more than 80% of employee IT support requests across 70+ deployments, with up to ~50% ITSM licensing cost reduction and large-enterprise savings on the order of 5M+ USD annually. PagerGPT-style benchmarks cite cost per interaction falling from roughly 6–40 USD (human-assisted) to about 0.50–0.70 USD via self-service agents. Case compilations cite 30–45% productivity gains and 50–80% error reductions in some knowledge-work processes.
  5. Adoption is broad; maturity varies. McKinsey’s 2024 State of AI finds 65% of organizations regularly using generative AI and 72% with AI deployed in at least one function. Arcade’s 2025 agentic analysis reports 79% with some AI agent implementation, 23% scaling, 39% experimenting. Benchmarks cited in industry commentary place a substantial minority of Fortune 500 companies running agents in production by early 2026.
  6. Pricing is shifting from seats toward usage and outcomes. LangGraph’s model combines per-node execution fees with standby time; agent platforms often emphasize execution volume and concurrency over seat count. Commentary highlights outcome-based and per-action models as emerging standards versus classic per-user SaaS.
  7. Barriers are organizational and risk-driven: skills gaps, integration difficulty, security, data protection, and governance for autonomous execution—not lack of interest.

3. Budget Shift (2025–2026)

  • •GenAI vs SaaS market: VC analysis tags ~37B USD enterprise GenAI spend in 2025 as on the order of ~6% of global SaaS over a three-year window—mid-single-digit reallocation of incremental software budget toward GenAI (often agentic or semi-agentic).
  • •AI share of core IT: IDC predicts G2000 firms allocating over 40% of core IT spend to AI initiatives by 2025, with intelligent automation and AI systems growing far faster than aggregate IT spend.
  • •Agentic share of AI budget: Arcade (2025) finds 43% of companies allocating more than half of AI budgets to agentic systems.

Interpretation: Public data still lacks a clean “% of SaaS formally shifted to AaaS” series; the best read is low-double-digit directional reallocation into agentic deployments alongside faster-growing AI-centric totals.

5. Industry & Company Size (Where Data Exists)

McKinsey highlights banking, high tech, and life sciences among industries with large genAI impact potential; 72% of organizations had AI in at least one function by 2024. Agentic use cases cluster in customer service, supply chain, and IT operations; manufacturing shows strong reported growth in agent adoption for predictive maintenance and quality. 6. Barriers to AaaS Adoption

  • •Skills & integration: ~1/3 of enterprise AI leaders cite skills as top obstacle; ~28% struggle integrating genAI into existing stacks (Forrester 2024 highlights).
  • •Security & data: Enterprise surveys report very high shares (often ~90%+) concerned about security and data protection for AI applications.
  • •Governance: Analysts stress new models for data access, BYOAI, and autonomous external action.

Deep Analysis

1. Are AaaS Subscriptions on Track to Capture 30%+ of Enterprise Software Spend by 2028?

No major analyst publishes AaaS as a standalone wallet share, so the 30%-by-2028 claim must be triangulated from enterprise application revenue, SaaS totals, AI-centric spend, and GenAI budget growth. Global SaaS revenue near ~399B USD in 2024, with forward growth, implies a 2028 SaaS market in the hundreds of billions to low trillions depending on definition. IDC’s enterprise application forecast and AI-centric spending guide show AI lines compounding much faster than aggregate IT. GenAI spend rises from tens of billions toward larger totals in commentary.

The open question is how much of AI-centric and GenAI spend is true AaaS (autonomous workflow execution, usage/outcome pricing) versus API access, infra, or embedded assistants. Gartner’s long-range agentic revenue share anchor (~30% of enterprise application revenue by 2035) supports a path where double-digit shares by 2028 are plausible under aggressive interpolation, but published 2026 data cannot validate an exact 30% AaaS wallet share or cleanly separate “AaaS” from “agentic SaaS” in financial reporting.

2. TCO Dynamics — When Do AI Agents Beat SaaS Economics by 40–50%?

Case evidence is strongest in IT support and high-volume customer service: labor plus seat-based tools replaced by agent execution and orchestration, with documented per-interaction and licensing savings. Gains come from substituting seats and manual handling with compute plus orchestration, and from fewer errors and rework.

Uniform 40–50% TCO improvement across whole software estates is not established: integration, compliance, observability, and legacy coupling create front-loaded cost, and incumbents may embed agents and capture part of the value through repriced bundles.

3. Pricing Models Signal the Shift from SaaS to AaaS

Seat-based SaaS monetizes access; AaaS monetizes work completed—per node, per action, per outcome. LangGraph-style per-node pricing, tiered agent platforms scaling on concurrency and execution volume, and ITSM vendors positioning agents as license-cost reducers all point the same direction: buyers optimize unit economics of work, not seat counts.

That compresses the traditional SaaS margin on under-utilized seats and pushes value capture toward whoever operates the execution layer—often while systems of record remain as SaaS underneath.

Implications for Tech Companies

  1. Re-segment around end-to-end workflows where agents can own measurable outcomes (IT tickets, bookings, cases), not feature checklists that still require manual execution.
  2. Move pricing toward execution and outcomes—hybrid platform fees plus indexed revenue on tasks completed, tickets resolved, or time saved.
  3. Invest in orchestration, monitoring, and governance as first-class product surface area alongside traditional access control and audit.
  4. Prioritize categories with proven ROI—service desks, CX, and high-volume back-office where benchmarks already exist.
  5. Plan for hybrid estates: SaaS as system of record, agents as the experience and orchestration layer—who owns orchestration owns a growing share of marginal value.

Implications for Tech Professionals

  1. Build agent orchestration and workflow design skills, not only single-turn prompting.
  2. Bias toward domains with production agent adoption—customer service, IT operations, manufacturing automation—where demand signals are strongest.
  3. Master ROI language: cost per ticket, FTE equivalents, error rates, and cycle time.
  4. Pair technical depth with security, compliance, and data governance, especially in regulated sectors.
  5. Align your narrative with outcome metrics as employers shift from shipping features to delivering measurable operational results.
