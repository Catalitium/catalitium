Section 1 — The Software Industry Shift

SaaS market dynamics, pricing shifts, AI-native VC flows, and the agent platform rise

SaaS Market: Growth vs. Slowdown

Global SaaS market: approximately $358 billion in 2024. Gartner projects SaaS spend to reach ~$300 billion in end-user spend by 2025. SaaS pricing rose approximately 11.4% year-over-year in 2025 versus 2.7% average market inflation — a signal that AI-embedded products command premium pricing even as legacy seat-based SaaS faces displacement.

Rise of AI-Native Software

  • •AI startups raised $110B in 2024 — a 62% increase over 2023 — while non-AI startup funding declined 12% (Dealroom / TechCrunch)
  • •GenAI companies specifically raised $47.4B in 2024
  • •AI now absorbs approximately one-third of all global VC funding
  • •48% of all US developer tool VC deals in 2024 were AI-powered (Evil Martians)

SaaS Pricing Model Shift

  • •61% of SaaS companies used some form of usage-based pricing by 2022 (OpenView)
  • •Gartner projected 30%+ of enterprise SaaS to incorporate outcome-based components by 2025
  • •Forrester: 60%+ of SaaS providers will offer consumption-based pricing by 2025
  • •Hybrid pricing models report 38% higher net revenue retention (OpenView 2023)

Catalitium Interpretation

The SaaS market is not dying — it is bifurcating and repricing. Horizontal, seat-based SaaS is under pressure from both AI-native replacements and AI-powered enterprise deals that command significantly higher contract values. The shift to usage and outcome-based pricing is structural: as AI delivers measurable outcomes, buyers will increasingly refuse to pay for seats that may not be utilized.

Section 3 — Changing Hiring Demand

Developer market collapse, AI role explosion, and layoffs linked to AI

Software Developer Job Market Decline

  • •Software developer postings on Indeed: 65% of Jan 2020 levels — a 35% decline and 3.5x fewer than mid-2022 peak
  • •2024: slowest year-over-year rate of new software developer jobs in the US on record (Technical.ly, June 2025)
  • •Tech unemployment peaked at 5.7% in February 2025 — above the national average
  • •New graduates: just ~7% of hires at big tech firms, down 25% from 2023 (LinkedIn, June 2025)
  • •Compared to all jobs (+10% since 2020), software development is the worst-performing major occupation category at −34%

AI Role Explosion

  • •AI Engineer: #1 fastest growing job title on LinkedIn for 2025 and again for 2026
  • •AI/ML engineer roles growing 13.1% quarter-over-quarter and 41.8% year-over-year (Aura 2025)
  • •AI-related job postings on LinkedIn: +38% between 2020 and 2024
  • •US roles requiring AI literacy: 70% YoY increase (WEF/LinkedIn, January 2026)
  • •AI has created 1.3 million new roles globally, including 600,000+ data center jobs

Tech Layoffs Linked to AI

  • •184,000 global tech layoffs in 2025 (through October); 27.3% (~50,000) directly linked to AI (Silicon Valley Business Journal)
  • •Challenger, Gray & Christmas: 48,414 AI-cited US job cuts in 2025; 31,039 in October alone
  • •Salesforce: 5,000 layoffs primarily in customer service as AI agents handle volume
  • •Microsoft: 19,215 layoffs in 2025 while simultaneously prioritizing AI investments

Catalitium Interpretation

The hiring data reveals a structural substitution effect in real time: traditional developer roles are declining at a historic rate while AI specialist roles grow faster than any job category. This is not a cycle — it started precisely with ChatGPT’s release in early 2023 and has accelerated with each generation of AI coding tools. The same companies laying off general software engineers are competing aggressively to hire AI engineers at premium salaries.

Section 4 — The New Software Workforce

Non-engineers building software and the solo founder surge

Non-Engineers Building Software

  • •Gartner: low-code application development accounted for 65%+ of all app development activity as of 2024
  • •Gartner predicted citizen developers would outnumber traditional developers 4-to-1 in large organizations by 2025
  • •70% of new enterprise apps to use low-code or no-code technologies by 2025 (Gartner)
  • •Global no-code/low-code market expected to reach $35.22B by 2030

Solo Builders and the Micro-Founder Surge

  • •Base44: solo developer built AI coding tool to 250,000 users in 6 months; acquired by Wix for $80M (July 2025)
  • •API.market: solo founder built marketplace to 3,100 customers and $200K ARR before expanding to 3 people
  • •LinkedIn “founder” title: 58% increase in users adding the label between July 2024 and July 2025 — more than triple 2022 figures
  • •30%+ of professionals report AI has increased their likelihood of starting their own business (LinkedIn/HR Leader, Jan 2026)

Catalitium Interpretation

The democratization of software creation is real and accelerating. An enormous number of people who could not previously build software are now capable of creating and shipping products. This expands the addressable market for developer tools while simultaneously compressing demand for traditional junior engineering roles. More software will be built in 2026 than ever before — by fewer professional engineers.

Section 7 — Risks, Limitations, and the Klarna Warning

Developer trust data, governance gaps, and a cautionary case study

AI Reliability and Developer Trust

  • •Stack Overflow 2025: only 33% of developers trust AI accuracy; 46% actively distrust it
  • •66% of developers say AI gives solutions that are “almost right, but not quite”
  • •45% report debugging AI-generated code takes more time than expected
  • •76% refuse to use AI for deployment and monitoring; 69% refuse for project planning

Governance and Security Gaps

  • •63% of organizations lack AI governance initiatives (IBM Cost of Data Breach Report 2025)
  • •Shadow AI increases data breach costs by $670K per incident (IBM 2025)
  • •Nearly 40% of organizations operate with ad hoc or no AI-specific governance (Acuvity 2025)
  • •Only 20% of firms have mature governance models for agentic AI autonomy (Deloitte 2026)

The Klarna Cautionary Tale

Case Study: Klarna — Cost Optimization Without Service Redesign

  • ›Reduced workforce 40%: from 5,527 to ~3,000 employees; AI chatbot replaced 700 customer service reps
  • ›Projected $40M annual profit improvement; 73% increase in revenue per employee
  • ›BUT: customer satisfaction plummeted; quality became erratic; Klarna began rehiring humans (Fast Company, Jan 2026)
  • ›CEO Sebastian Siemiatkowski: “Cost unfortunately seems to have been too predominant an evaluation factor… lower quality.”

Catalitium Interpretation

The Klarna case should be required reading for every executive considering AI-driven workforce reduction. The headline metrics were real — 40% fewer employees, 73% higher RPE, faster resolution times. But the unmeasured cost — customer satisfaction, brand trust, service quality — was also real. The lesson is not “do not use AI for customer service.” It is “do not use AI as a cost-cutting tool without redesigning the service model around it.” Speed without quality is a product failure, not an AI success.

Catalitium Recommendations

For Job Seekers & Developers

  • ›Treat AI fluency as table stakes. 84% of developers use AI tools — demonstrate you use them better, not just that you use them.
  • ›Target the 56% wage premium directly. Six focused months of AI tooling mastery is worth more than a second degree.
  • ›Build production evidence, not course certificates. Employers pay for engineers who deploy AI in real systems.
  • ›Do not wait for the traditional entry-level market to recover. Use contract work, open source, and building in public as alternative entry points.
  • ›Learn where AI underperforms. The METR finding (AI slows experienced devs on complex codebases) means critical evaluation is a career asset.

For Recruiters & Talent Teams

  • ›Audit junior role descriptions for tasks AI can complete in hours. If Cursor can do it, you are sourcing for a role that will not exist in 18 months.
  • ›Build AI fluency into technical screens. Include at least one task requiring AI tool use — and demonstrated judgment about its output.
  • ›Recalibrate salary bands for AI-skilled roles now. The 56% wage premium gap means you are almost certainly underbanding.
  • ›Do not eliminate all junior hiring. The AWS CEO’s warning — replacing juniors with AI is “one of the dumbest things you can do” — points to the pipeline problem that surfaces in 3–5 years.

For Companies & Founders

  • ›Benchmark your team against AI-native unit economics. Series A average is 15 people (down from 22 in 2022). If you are hiring at 2019 rates, you are building in structural cost risk.
  • ›Do not use AI as a blunt cost-cutting instrument. Klarna is the lesson. Measure quality outcomes, not just headcount reduction and RPE.
  • ›Close the adoption-to-transformation gap. You are almost certainly in the 88% who use AI — identify whether you are in the 6% who are transformed.
  • ›Invest in the governance layer before scaling agentic AI. 63% lack governance while deploying aggressively. Every $670K shadow-AI breach is avoidable.
  • ›Build a deliberate apprenticeship pipeline even as you adopt AI. Senior engineers do not grow in a vacuum. If you stop hiring juniors entirely, the talent supply dries up for your competitors’ benefit.

Closing Statement

The transformation is not coming. It is underway.

Software developer job postings are at a five-year low. AI-native companies are generating 10–20x more revenue per employee than traditional SaaS businesses. GitHub Copilot alone is now generating more revenue than GitHub was worth when Microsoft acquired it.

The data does not support either extreme: AI is not destroying tech jobs in aggregate, and it is not leaving everything unchanged. What it is doing is bifurcating. At the top: scarcity, premium salaries, and a 56% wage premium for AI-skilled talent. At the bottom: historic collapse in entry-level demand, rising unemployment among recent CS graduates, and the lean team model resetting what software companies are supposed to cost.

The companies that navigate this well will do two things simultaneously: capture the AI productivity premium at the senior layer, and invest deliberately in the apprenticeship pathways that ensure the next generation of senior engineers actually exists.
