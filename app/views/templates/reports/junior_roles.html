Executive Summary

The global tech workforce is not shrinking — it is restructuring. Total US tech employment grew 1.2% in 2024 and is up 12.2% since 2021. But this aggregate growth masks a structural collapse at the bottom of the career ladder. Entry-level tech job postings have dropped more than 35% since January 2023. For roles with high AI exposure, the decline exceeds 40%.

At the same time, demand for senior and AI-skilled talent is accelerating. AI engineers averaged $206,000 in 2025 — a $50,000 increase in a single year. PwC’s AI skill salary premium doubled from 25% to 56% in one year. GitHub Copilot reached 20 million users with 90% Fortune 100 adoption, writing 46% of the average user’s code.

Catalitium Interpretation

This is not a tech job crisis. It is a tech job bifurcation. The market is splitting into two tracks: AI-augmented senior roles commanding record premiums, and entry-level positions being compressed by AI productivity. The on-ramp is narrowing — not disappearing — but the timing is brutal for 2025 and 2026 graduates.

CS bachelor’s degrees more than doubled over the past decade to 112,720 in 2022–2023 — the highest increase of any major — just as entry-level demand collapsed. CS graduate unemployment reached 6.1% in 2024. Series A startup team sizes dropped from 22 to 15 employees between 2022 and 2024.

1. Global Tech Employment: What the Aggregates Hide

US tech occupation employment reached approximately 5.9 million workers in 2024, projected to reach 6.1 million in 2025. Net tech employment stood at 9.6 million — essentially flat year-over-year. These headline numbers look stable. What they hide is a structural shift in who is being hired.

High-tech industry tech talent workforce actually shrank by 0.7% in 2024, even as overall tech employment grew. The growth is concentrated in AI-native roles, platform engineering, and senior positions — not in traditional junior entry points.

2. The Entry Level Collapse

Entry-level job postings in the US declined approximately 35% since January 2023 — over 100,000 fewer new monthly job postings, according to Revelio Labs data from August 2025. The pattern is not random. Roles with high AI exposure saw drops exceeding 40%.

Meta, Google, and Amazon reduced P1 and P2 (junior) hiring rates by 73% relative to their 2021 peak. Simultaneously, these companies grew headcount in Staff+ and Principal engineer categories. The industry is restructuring around fewer, more senior people capable of leveraging AI tooling.

Catalitium Interpretation

Entry-level roles dropped because junior work — writing boilerplate, generating tests, scaffolding APIs — can now be delegated to AI. Companies do not need as many people to do the volume of output that previously required large junior cohorts. This is structural, not cyclical.

3. The Senior Premium Effect

While junior demand collapses, senior and AI-skilled talent is experiencing the opposite dynamic. AI engineers earned an average of $206,000 in 2025 — a $50,000 increase from the prior year. Senior AI engineers exceed $300,000 total compensation at large tech companies.

The METR Contrarian Finding

A July 2025 METR study found that experienced developers working on familiar, complex codebases took 19% longer with AI tools than without. This is consistent with the broader data: AI productivity gains are largest in greenfield work and boilerplate-heavy tasks. In highly contextual, architecturally complex systems, AI adds overhead. Senior engineers on legacy codebases should not expect 55% gains.

5. Hiring Behavior Shift: The Lean Team Model

The organizational response to AI productivity gains is not to hire more people. It is to need fewer. Average software startup team size at Series A dropped from 22 employees in 2022 to 15 employees in 2024, according to Carta — a 32% reduction in two years. Investor expectations are now built around this lean model.

Duolingo cut its contractor workforce by 10% in January 2024, directly citing AI as the replacement. Salesforce announced a hiring pause for software engineers in 2025, with Marc Benioff stating AI agents eliminated the need for additional headcount. These are not isolated events — they reflect a recalibrated model of how much human labor is needed per unit of software output.

6. Education Pipeline Misalignment

CS bachelor’s degrees more than doubled over the past decade: from 51,696 in 2013–14 to 112,720 in 2022–23 — the highest increase in bachelor’s degree earners of any major, at 4.3% year-over-year growth, even as overall bachelor’s degrees declined 3.0%. The education system responded to the “learn to code” moment of the 2010s. The market that awaited those graduates is fundamentally different.

CS graduate unemployment hit 6.1% in 2024 (NACE) — up from 3.9% in 2022. Median starting salary for new CS graduates dropped 5% in real terms between 2022 and 2024. The supply-demand scissors are closing precisely when the largest graduating cohort in history is entering the market.

7. Expert Signals and Forward Outlook

“AI could eliminate up to 50% of entry-level office jobs within five years. Unemployment could spike to 10–20%. Most of them are unaware this is about to happen.”

— Dario Amodei, CEO, Anthropic (May 2025)

“In 2025, we may see the first AI that can do the work of a mid-level software engineer.”

— Sam Altman, CEO, OpenAI (January 2025)

“30% of Microsoft’s code is now written by AI.”

— Satya Nadella, CEO, Microsoft (October 2024)

WEF Future of Jobs Report 2025 projects 92 million jobs displaced and 170 million created by 2030 — a net gain of 78 million globally. AI and information processing technologies will create 19 million jobs while displacing 9 million. But the 10-million displacement will be concentrated at the junior and entry-level end.

The Bureau of Labor Statistics projects 15% growth for software developers from 2024 to 2034 — faster than average. But that growth is concentrated at the senior and specialized end of the distribution, not in the entry-level roles that have historically absorbed new graduates.

9. Catalitium Recommendations

For Job Seekers

  • ›Stop applying to 2021-era junior roles. Reframe around AI-augmented output.
  • ›Target the AI skill premium directly. PwC’s 56% is real — 6 months of focused AI tooling beats a second degree.
  • ›Show work. Build and deploy with Claude, Cursor, or Copilot. Outcomes over credentials.
  • ›Use non-traditional entry points: open source, contract work, building in public.
  • ›Be the developer who knows when the AI is wrong — critical evaluation is a career asset.

For Recruiters

  • ›Audit junior role descriptions. If they describe work AI can do in hours, the role won’t exist in 18 months.
  • ›Build AI fluency assessment into technical screens. Include a task where candidates use AI — and demonstrate judgment about the output.
  • ›Recalibrate salary bands for AI-skilled roles before losing candidates.
  • ›Create “AI augmented junior” tracks that hire for judgment and learning velocity, not task execution.

For Companies

  • ›Lean team model is locked in. Build hiring plans around AI-augmented productivity from day one.
  • ›Invest in upskilling existing mid-level engineers on AI tooling before external hiring.
  • ›Create internal AI tooling programs. Your best pipeline is engineers already familiar with your codebase.
  • ›Don’t eliminate all junior hiring. The pipeline to senior talent dries up in 5 years if you do.

Conclusion: The Ladder Hasn’t Disappeared — Its Bottom Rung Has

The data in this report describes a market in the middle of a structural transition, not at the end of one. Total tech employment continues to grow. AI skills are being rewarded at unprecedented rates. Senior and specialized roles are holding up.

But the entry level — the on-ramp that every senior engineer once traveled — is under severe pressure. The CS degree that reliably launched careers for a decade is now producing graduates into the worst entry-level market since the dot-com correction.

The lean team model is being locked in at the Series A stage, with investor expectations built around it. This is not a temporary dip waiting to recover. The ladder hasn’t disappeared — its bottom rung has been replaced by a higher bar. The question for individuals and institutions is whether they adapt faster than the market moves.
