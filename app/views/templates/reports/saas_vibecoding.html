Executive Summary

The global SaaS market — valued at $315–$408 billion in 2025 — is not collapsing. But its internal architecture is cracking at the foundation. While headline numbers still grow, the structural forces have shifted: AI-native companies grow at 4.3× the rate of traditional SaaS. Vibecoding tools like Cursor went from $1M to $1B ARR in 24 months. $300 billion was erased from SaaS market caps in a single day in February 2026. The build vs. buy equation — stable for two decades — has permanently changed. This is not a SaaS problem. It is a business model problem.

  • SaaS annual growth decelerated from 36% to ~12% over a decade and is forecast to continue falling [Aventis Advisors]
  • 35% of companies have already replaced at least one SaaS tool with a custom AI build; 78% plan to build more in 2026 [Retool, Feb 2026]
  • Cursor: $1M → $1B ARR in 24 months — widely considered the fastest $1M-to-$1B in software history [Sacra]
  • GitHub Copilot: 20M users, 90% Fortune 100 adoption, generates 46% of average user's code [GitHub/Microsoft Q4 FY25]
  • YC AI-native cohort share: 25% (Winter 2025) → 88% (Summer 2025) — exponential, not linear [YC/Snyk]
  • AI-native SaaS: 100% median growth rate, 132% NRR vs. 23% growth / 108% NRR for traditional SaaS [Emergence Capital]
  • Build cost for an internal tool: $5K–$42K in 2–4 weeks vs. $180K–$350K agency over 12 weeks — a 70–90% collapse
  • $19.8M in SaaS license waste per enterprise annually; average enterprise runs 447 apps with only 47–53% utilized [Zylo 2026]
  • SaaS valuation multiples compressed: 15–20× ARR (2021) → 6–10× ARR (2025) [Altimeter/SaaStr]
  • Deloitte projects AI agent solutions could represent 60% of the total software market by 2030

SaaS Market: Size, Growth, and Stress Fractures

The numbers are still large. Fortune Business Insights values the global SaaS market at $315.68 billion in 2025, with projections reaching $1.48 trillion by 2034. Precedence Research places it higher at $408 billion. Gartner — the conservative voice — pegged end-user SaaS spending at $247 billion in 2024. All three agree on direction: the market is still growing.

Large numbers obscure a harder truth. The growth engine is losing pressure. SaaS expansion rates collapsed from 36% annually to approximately 12% over the past decade, with analysts at Aventis Advisors projecting continued deceleration to 11% and below. Public SaaS revenue multiples tell the same story: the 15–20× ARR of the 2021 peak has halved to 6–10× by 2025. Maturity, for businesses built on hypergrowth assumptions, is its own crisis.

The startup layer is feeling it harder. Carta data shows 966 startups shut down on their platform in 2024 — up 25.6% from 2023. Enterprise SaaS companies made up 32% of those shutdowns. Vibecoding: Definition, Scale, and Adoption

The term "vibecoding" was coined by Andrej Karpathy on February 2, 2025. By year end, Collins Dictionary named it their Word of the Year. One year later, Karpathy called it a "throwaway tweet" and proposed "agentic engineering" as the more precise professional term — but the cultural moment had already arrived.

"There's a new kind of coding I call 'vibe coding', where you fully give in to the vibes, embrace exponentials, and forget that the code even exists... I 'Accept All' always, I don't read the diffs anymore."

— Andrej Karpathy, X (formerly Twitter), February 2, 2025

The adoption story is not about recreational code generation. It is about a structural shift in who can build software — and at what cost. GitHub Copilot alone has 20 million cumulative users, 90% Fortune 100 adoption, and generates 46% of the average user's code (61% for Java). Satya Nadella stated on Microsoft's Q4 FY25 earnings that Copilot is now a larger business than GitHub itself was at its $7.5 billion acquisition.

The disruption is not being driven by vibe coders throwing prompts at walls. It is being driven by mainstream developers who are now 55% faster at task completion, shipping 61% more code, and choosing to build internal tools instead of buying SaaS subscriptions.

Catalitium's read:

The real story is not "anyone can code now." It is "the cost of building software has dropped 70–90%, permanently." That economic shift is what threatens SaaS — not the cultural phenomenon of vibecoding.

The Build vs. Buy Equation Has Broken

For decades, building custom software had a clear answer: buy. SaaS made it affordable to access best-in-class tools without bearing development cost. That logic is now breaking down at its foundation. Retool surveyed 817 builders in their February 2026 Build vs. Buy report: 35% have already replaced at least one SaaS tool with a custom-built alternative. 78% plan to build more in 2026.

Winners: The AI Coding Tool Economy

Every disruption produces winners. As traditional SaaS subscription revenue leaks to custom-built alternatives, value is concentrating in a new layer: AI developer infrastructure. The AI code tools market grew from effectively zero in 2023 to $7.37 billion in 2025 — projected at $23.97 billion by 2030 at a 26.6% CAGR. That is more than twice the growth rate of the market it is disrupting.

The VC signal is unambiguous. AI captured approximately 50% of all global venture capital funding in 2025: $202.3 billion total, up 75%+ from $114 billion in 2024. Foundation model companies alone raised $80 billion. This is capital fleeing traditional SaaS and flowing into the infrastructure that replaces it.

Below the infrastructure layer, a new economy is forming. Solo developers are launching profitable micro-SaaS products in days using AI-powered stacks. "Software composers" — non-traditional developers from business and operations roles — are building production tools via natural language prompts. The barrier to building a software product has never been lower in the history of computing.

Catalitium's Read

The seat-based SaaS business model is already dead. Most companies just have not been told yet. When one AI agent replaces 5–10 human seats, the entire revenue equation collapses. Usage-based companies grow 31% faster. 85% of SaaS leaders have adopted usage-based models. Salesforce launched Agentforce specifically to get ahead of this. The transition from seats to outcomes is not a prediction — it is already in earnings calls.

Counterpoint: SaaS Evolves, Not Dies

The headline "SaaS is Dead" — used by a16z in their January 2026 report — is directionally true but narratively imprecise. A $315–$408 billion market does not cease to exist. What is happening is more specific: horizontal, commoditized, thin-moat SaaS is being structurally disrupted. Vertical, AI-native, deep-workflow SaaS is actually accelerating.

Legacy players are not standing still. Salesforce launched Agentforce — autonomous AI agents that explicitly acknowledge the end of per-seat logic. ServiceNow's Q3 2025 revenue hit $3.41B (+22% YoY) with AI-related contract value approaching $500M. Google's Sundar Pichai addressed the "SaaS is dead" narrative directly on Q4 2025 earnings: "95% of the top 20 SaaS companies are using Gemini."

A METR randomized controlled trial (July 2025) found that experienced developers took 19% longer on familiar, complex codebases with AI tools — suggesting productivity gains are concentrated in greenfield builds and less expert contexts. 85% of SaaS spend goes to renewals. Deep switching costs and workflow integration remain powerful retention moats for incumbent tools.

The correct frame:

Commodity SaaS is being structurally disrupted. AI-native SaaS with genuine workflow depth is experiencing the best growth conditions in the industry's history. Know which one your tools are.

Forward Outlook: 2026–2030

The forward projections are unusually aligned across institutions with different methodologies and incentives. Deloitte's 2026 Global Software Industry Outlook projects AI agent-powered solutions could represent 60% of the total addressable software market by 2030. Gartner forecasts enterprise software spend rising at least 40% by 2027. AI-enabled application spend hit $644 billion in 2025 — up 76.4% from 2024.

The competitive landscape for software is entering a compression phase: fewer, stronger players with genuine AI differentiation versus a long tail of undifferentiated tools being absorbed or abandoned.

The companies that will define the next software era are building now. They are AI-native by architecture, not by press release. They price on outcomes, not seats. They deploy faster than their competitors can procure. The window for first-mover advantage in this transition is 18–36 months.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is SaaS actually dying or just changing shape?

Not dying in aggregate — the global SaaS market is valued at $315–$408 billion and still growing. What is dying is a specific category: horizontal, seat-based, undifferentiated SaaS without AI-native capabilities. AI-native SaaS is growing at 4.3× the rate of traditional SaaS, with 100% median growth vs. 23%.

Will every company start building instead of buying SaaS?

No. 35% have already replaced at least one SaaS tool — not all of them. Building wins for commodity and internal tools. SaaS still wins for complex compliance workflows, multi-organization platforms, deeply regulated verticals, and tools where the moat is proprietary data or network effects rather than the interface.

How do we know if our SaaS product is replaceable by vibecoding?

Ask three questions: (1) Can the core workflow be described in under 500 words? (2) Does our value come from the interface, or from proprietary data and integrations? (3) Would a developer with Cursor and four weeks deliver 80% of our value? If yes to all three, your category is structurally exposed.

What should enterprises track quarterly to know if this is hitting their category?

Track: renewal pushback rate from internal stakeholders; build-vs-buy requests from your engineering team; number of internal tools deployed vs. the same period last year; SaaS spend as a percentage of headcount (it should be declining). Also watch whether your top SaaS vendors are announcing pricing model changes — that signals they are feeling the pressure.

What is "Software as Labor" and why does it matter?

a16z's framing for AI that delivers outcomes rather than tools. Instead of buying a seat in a platform, you buy a result. Salesforce's Agentforce is the first mainstream commercial implementation. Gartner projects 30%+ of enterprise SaaS will incorporate outcome-based components. This is the business model transition that makes per-seat pricing obsolete — not vibecoding per se, but the AI agent layer above it.
